The Reviewer agent has a hard rule: **cite at least one
scholarly source per disputed verse.** This page is the
canonical list of acceptable and unacceptable sources.

The list is not exhaustive — any peer-reviewed scholarly work
of recognised quality is acceptable. The list below is the
reviewer's default reach.

## Hebrew Bible

### Lexicons

- **BDB** — Brown, Driver & Briggs, *A Hebrew and English Lexicon
  of the Old Testament* (Oxford, 1906; reprinted Hendrickson).
  Public Domain. The default Hebrew lexicon for translation
  work; older but exhaustively cited.
- **HALOT** — Koehler, Baumgartner, Stamm, *The Hebrew and
  Aramaic Lexicon of the Old Testament* (Brill, 1994–2000). The
  current scholarly standard; modernised semantic ranges.
- **NIDOTTE** — Willem A. VanGemeren (ed.), *New International
  Dictionary of Old Testament Theology and Exegesis* (Zondervan,
  1997). Word-study reference; useful for theologically loaded
  terms.
- **TDOT** — Botterweck, Ringgren, Fabry (eds.), *Theological
  Dictionary of the Old Testament* (Eerdmans, 1974–2018, 17
  vols.). Comprehensive theological-philological lexicon.
- **THAT / TLOT** — Jenni & Westermann (eds.), *Theological
  Lexicon of the Old Testament* (Hendrickson, 1997). One-volume
  theological lexicon.

## Arabic / Qur'an

### Lexicons

- **Lane** — Edward William Lane, *An Arabic-English Lexicon*
  (8 vols, 1863–1893). Public Domain. The standard classical
  Arabic lexicon for serious work.
- **Hava** — J. G. Hava, *Arabic-English Dictionary for the
  Use of Students* (Catholic Press, 1899). Public Domain.
- **Wehr-Cowan** — Hans Wehr, *A Dictionary of Modern Written
  Arabic* (ed. Cowan, Spoken Language Services, 1976; current
  4th ed., 1994). Modern reference; copyrighted.

### Tafsīr (classical commentaries)

- **al-Tabari**, *Tafsir al-Tabari* (9th–10th c.). The
  foundational tafsīr; Arabic.
- **al-Zamakhshari**, *al-Kashshaf* (12th c.). Mu'tazilite
  linguistic tafsīr.
- **al-Razi**, *Mafatih al-Ghayb* (12th–13th c.). Major
  philosophical tafsīr.
- **Ibn Kathir**, *Tafsir al-Qur'an al-Azim* (14th c.). Widely
  cited Sunni tafsīr.

### Modern scholarship

- **Madigan**, *The Qur'an's Self-Image* (Princeton, 2001).
- **Saleh**, *The Formation of the Classical Tafsīr Tradition*
  (Brill, 2004).
- **Reynolds**, *The Qur'an and the Bible: Text and Commentary*

## Raëlian canon (when the WoH lens is the explicit source)

These are not philological references — they are the project's
foundational canon. The Reviewer may cite them when the WoH
reading explicitly rests on a canon passage:

- **TBWTT** — *The Book Which Tells The Truth* (Vorilhon, 1974).
  Cited as `TBWTT ch. N` or `TBWTT p. N`.
- **ETTMTTP** — *Extra-Terrestrials Took Me To Their Planet*
  (Vorilhon, 1975). Cited as `ETTMTTP ch. N`.
- **LWTE** — *Let's Welcome The Extra-Terrestrials* (Vorilhon,
  1979). Cited as `LWTE ch. N`.

The Reviewer should cite both the canon source AND a
philological source on any verse where both inform the
decision. A glossary entry that rests on the canon alone is a
`claim_type: speculative` candidate by definition.

## Unacceptable sources

The Reviewer must not cite any of these as the sole basis for
defending a translation choice:

- **The Wheel of Heaven lens "supports it."** Not a citation;
  this is what the project is trying to defend, not what
  defends it.
- **Popular ancient-astronaut literature.** Sitchin, von
  Däniken, Tellinger, popular ancient-aliens authors are not
  scholarly sources. They may be cited as cultural references in
  long-form articles, but not as defences in a translation
  decision.
- **Wikipedia.** Acceptable for orientation but not for
  defending lexical decisions.
- **The project's own wiki entries.** Wiki entries are
  downstream of these decisions, not upstream. Citing them
  creates circular justification.
- **Religious devotional commentaries.** A devotional commentary
  written for a specific tradition's spiritual practice (Baxter
  on Job, Henry on Genesis, Spurgeon on Psalms, classical
  Tafsīr Ibn Kathir on the Qur'an's miracles) is not
  philological-scholarly work. May be cited for *what a
  tradition says*; not for *what the text means*.
- **Anonymous internet sources.** Quora, Reddit, YouTube
  videos, blog posts without scholarly credentials.

## A worked example of a Reviewer citation

For a verse where the Editor chose to render *ruach* as "breath"
rather than "Spirit" (Gen 1:2), an acceptable Reviewer citation
might read:

> **Verdict:** approve.
> **Citations:** BDB s.v. רוּחַ III ("breath of the mouth or
> nostrils … breath of life"); Westermann, *Genesis 1–11*
> (1994), pp. 106–108 (argues for "wind/breath" reading on
> contextual grounds with the *merachefet* verb); NJPS
> *Tanakh* (1985 footnote acknowledging "or wind").
> **Reasoning:** The Hebrew lexical range supports breath /
> wind / spirit; *merachefet* (Deut 32:11) specifies aerial
> hovering, which fits the physical reading more naturally
> than the abstract; the lens-favoured reading is also the
> reading multiple scholarly sources defend.

That citation chain — BDB (lexicon), Westermann (commentary),
NJPS (translation tradition) — is the kind of grounding that
the Reviewer's verdict must rest on.
